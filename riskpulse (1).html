The Project

What is VaR and why does it matter?

Every bank's risk desk, every hedge fund PM, and every fintech platform that manages real money asks the same question every morning: "How much could we lose today?"

Value-at-Risk answers with a threshold. Expected Shortfall answers with the average loss beyond that threshold — the number that matters when a tail event actually hits. Basel III moved banks to CVaR precisely because VaR only tells you the door. CVaR tells you what's behind it.

Method 01

Parametric

Closed-form math under a Normal distribution assumption. Fast and analytical, but underestimates tail risk when returns are fat-tailed.

z = norm.ppf(1−α) · σ · √T
Method 02

Historical Simulation

Reads VaR/CVaR directly off actual past return data. No distributional assumption. Captures real fat tails, but blind to scenarios outside the lookback window.

VaR = Percentile(Losses, 95%)
Method 03 — Primary

Monte Carlo

50,000 correlated paths via Cholesky decomposition of the covariance matrix. Can model any distribution and stress conditions. The industry standard.

Σ = LLT → Z̃ = ZLT

Correlation Structure

Why correlation determines real risk.

Capital weight ≠ risk. The Cholesky decomposition uses this exact matrix to generate correlated simulated returns. TLT's negative correlation with equities provides genuine diversification — that's why it shows up as a small risk contributor despite a 15% weight.

During a 2008-style crisis, all off-diagonal correlations spike toward +1 — which is exactly what the stress-test engine replicates by adding +0.25 to every pair.
